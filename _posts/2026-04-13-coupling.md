I've been debating for a long time about whether I wanted to write this post or not but I've finally 
decided that after painstakingly trying to 'fix' a broken actuarial process, enough was enough and 
I needed to speak on the matter.

The problem I'm talking about is a very simple one on the surface but you'd be truly surprised just
how often I've encountered this problem in the wild. It goes a bit like this,

* We get some data from an external stakeholder, let's say in the form of a `.xlsx` file (and over an email)
* We write some shaky logic to process this data
* Next time, the stakeholder sends us a new version... but the data is structured differently (oh no)
* So we go into the codebase and rewrite parts of the code which read this data to fit the new column names
* We try to re-run our logic but it breaks because, of course, some of the business logic downstream - buried
 in a place you forgot to check - is still using the old column names / assumes the old structure and *that* needs to be updated as well
* A few months later, somebody asks you how the process works and you send them the documentation... only to discover
your documentation is based on a bygone version of said data and your stakeholder cannot connect the dots (and why should they?)

If you read through all of that, first of all: thank you for your attention; I promise I will repay you with some 
sage advice momentarily.

But second of all - and more to the point - what the hell is this?

You see, the problem with this process - though multi-faceted and complex to be sure - stems from a poor design 
decision made by the poor sod who was initially tasked with writing / running this process: namely highly 
intricate 'coupled' code.
